# Cadence And Mental Pages

Mental pages are an attempt to get the stabilizing effects of writing without
requiring paper. The core idea is that handwriting does more than record
thoughts: it slows generation, creates a feedback surface, forces compression,
and gives attention something stable to return to.

Whetstone can recreate parts of that loop internally or through lightweight
external anchors.

## Cadence Loop

The general cadence loop is:

1. Generate material.
2. Select the next phrase, handle, or claim.
3. Commit it to a mental or external surface.
4. Perceive it again.
5. Update the thread.

The important move is throttling. Fast thought generation can outrun
monitoring, selection, and memory. A cadence anchor slows the loop enough for
the controller to keep up.

## No-Paper Techniques

Useful variants include:

- breath-sentence cadence: one thought unit per breath;
- walking cadence: one handle or claim per repeated gait cycle;
- metronome or tapping cadence: a simple rhythm that prevents speed collapse;
- generator/editor separation: produce material first, then evaluate later;
- explain-to-wall: speak the current model as if teaching it;
- contradiction ping: periodically ask what would falsify the current frame;
- one-line mental ledger: keep the active claim, uncertainty, and next move.

These are not substitutes for notes when notes are available. They are
fallbacks for commutes, walks, showers, and moments when capturing too early
would break the thread.

## Mental Cache Lines

Mental cache lines are a small RAM protocol for preserving an idea long enough
to use it:

- handle: a short name for the idea;
- thesis: the compressed claim;
- next: the next micro-action or question;
- contrast: what the idea is not;
- image or somatic anchor: a nonverbal retrieval hook.

Refresh the cache line at natural waypoints. If the idea still matters after
the session, move it into Chronicle capture or a project note.

## Surroundings As Workspace

The Whetstone source note suggests treating surroundings as paper, method of
loci, timers, and stack frames. This is a useful direction as long as it stays
practical. A route, desk, room, or visual field can hold a sequence of handles,
but the goal is stable recall, not elaborate mnemonics for their own sake.

## Relationship To Chronicle

[Morning pages](../chronicle/morning-pages-and-life-os.md) use cadence to make
journaling possible in low-capture settings. Whetstone uses cadence to keep
problem-solving, planning, and creative thought from collapsing into
untracked drift.
