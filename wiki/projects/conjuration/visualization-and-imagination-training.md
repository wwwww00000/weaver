# Visualization And Imagination Training

Conjuration treats visualization as a trainable bridge between internal
imagination and external drawing. The goal is not just to have prettier
daydreams. The goal is to make imagined scenes stable enough to inspect,
rotate, inhabit, and translate into marks.

## Modes

The sources distinguish several useful imagery modes:

- first-person visualization: imagining from inside the scene, as if the
  visual field were present around the body;
- third-person or camera visualization: seeing oneself or a scene from an
  external viewpoint;
- abstract visualization: distorted, stylized, schematic, symbolic, or
  camera-effect imagery;
- spatial visualization: manipulating layouts, rotations, viewpoints, and
  object relations;
- object visualization: vivid color, texture, surface, and detail;
- multisensory visualization: adding touch, sound, weight, motion, and
  presence.

For Conjuration, first-person spatial visualization may be especially
important. It often feels easier to imagine an apple on the table in front of
you than to conjure a finished flat drawing of an apple. The practice should
use that embodied strength rather than bypass it.

## Core Exercises

Start with exercises that produce a visible check.

- Observe and recall: study an object, close the eyes or look away, reconstruct
  the image, then draw and compare.
- Image-to-field: imagine an object in the current visual field with accurate
  scale, lighting, and perspective.
- Viewpoint shift: move around an object physically, then imagine unseen angles
  and sketch them.
- Form rotation: rotate a cube, cylinder, hand, creature, or simple object in
  the mind before drawing.
- Scene patching: vaguely hold an entire scene, then flesh out one patch at a
  time instead of demanding a complete image at once.
- Dream or glimpse capture: sketch fleeting high-fidelity images before they
  disappear.

The notes warn that purely internal visualization practice can become vague.
Write the exercise goal before starting, record what was hard, and build in
some external check: observation, reference, drawing, or a 3D model.

## Image Translation

A central Conjuration skill is translating lived 3D space into a 2D image.
Useful tactics include:

- use a glasses frame, page edge, or imagined picture plane as a field-of-view
  guide;
- flatten envelopes, cross-sections, center lines, and bounding boxes before
  adding detail;
- visualize vanishing lines in the real visual field;
- use true-angle moments, floor distance, and body orientation to reason about
  perspective;
- project the current visual field as a smaller "video" on the page;
- reverse the process by reconstructing an immersive scene from a drawing.

This makes visualization a bidirectional practice: experience to page, and page
back to experience.

## Rebuilding Imagination

The imagination sources point to short daily exercises rather than heroic art
sessions:

- transform an everyday object into another object, creature, or machine;
- change one element of an ordinary scenario and explore the consequences;
- create a character and put them into an unusual constraint;
- alter a sensory experience and inhabit it;
- read or view evocative source material, then pretend to be somewhere else;
- use music, mood boards, or image collections as priming before drawing.

The practice should remain playful enough to revive childhood-style world
building, but constrained enough to improve a concrete skill.

## Liminal Imagery

Dreams, hypnagogic fragments, shower thoughts, naps, and bedtime drift can
supply unusual images or solutions. Conjuration should use them as source
material, not as a reason to damage sleep.

The small protocol is: load the theme gently, stop forcing, capture a handle
immediately afterward, and later decide whether the fragment wants to become a
sketch, scene, character, or wiki note.

## Open Questions

- Which visualization mode transfers most directly to drawing from imagination?
- How much of vivid imagery can be trained through external feedback rather
  than internal effort?
- Are fleeting high-fidelity glimpses a useful target, or a distraction from
  controllable low-fidelity construction?
- What should be tracked after each session: vividness, stability, perspective
  accuracy, line confidence, or recall?
