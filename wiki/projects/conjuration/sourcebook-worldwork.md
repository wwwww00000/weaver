# Sourcebook Worldwork

Sourcebook worldwork is constrained imaginative exploration. A sourcebook,
setting guide, art book, map, or worldbuilding text becomes a travel guide:
read a little, extract constraints, then inhabit the place through writing,
sketching, or solo play.

This is adjacent to solo tabletop roleplaying, but the goal is not necessarily
to play a full tactical game. The goal is to enter a world in a way that
generates scenes, characters, visual anchors, and creative momentum.

## Sourcebook As Travel Guide

Use source material at several levels of granularity:

- macro setting: genre, metaphysics, tone, history;
- region or city: geography, social structure, institutions;
- local site: tavern, shrine, ruin, station, estate, street, archive;
- factions: groups with pressure, motives, and conflict;
- NPCs: people with agendas, tells, and secrets;
- sensory anchors: smells, weather, textures, architecture, recurring sounds;
- mysteries: rumors, missing people, taboos, contradictions, locked doors.

The key move is to turn passive reading into a constraint database.

## Solo Exploration Loop

A lightweight session can look like this:

1. Read 2 to 6 pages of source material.
2. Extract one place, one faction, one NPC, one sensory anchor, and one mystery.
3. Drop a character or party into a concrete scene.
4. Ask an oracle question only when the outcome is genuinely uncertain.
5. Write each character's reaction before resolving the plot.
6. End with one unresolved thread.

This preserves surprise without needing a human game master. The oracle should
interrupt authorial drift, not replace judgment.

## Party Policies

Playing or writing as a party can make the world feel more autonomous. Give
each character a small policy:

- what they want;
- what they fear;
- default method;
- blind spot;
- what they notice first;
- what they refuse to ignore;
- one relationship to another party member.

Then ask what each character pushes for before deciding what "the story" does.
The scene becomes a collision of policies instead of a single authorial line.

## Three Useful Layers

The relevant solo-RPG materials can be treated as design patterns.

Journey-like tools emphasize place, texture, observation, and imaginative
tourism. Use them when the point is to inhabit a location and notice details.

Ironsworn-like tools emphasize vows, weak hits, progress, consequence, and
dramatic pressure. Use them when the worldwork needs stakes.

Worlds-Without-Number-like tools emphasize sandbox structure: factions,
regions, ruins, tags, threats, and local situations. Use them when source
material needs to become playable.

For Conjuration, the useful stack is: sandbox material creates the situation,
travelogue practice makes it vivid, and light oracle mechanics add friction.

## Relationship To Drawing

Sourcebook worldwork should feed visual practice. A scene log can produce:

- character design prompts;
- room, street, shrine, or ruin sketches;
- faction symbols and costume studies;
- creature construction exercises;
- maps and perspective scenes;
- manga panels or storyboard beats.

This keeps the writing side connected to Conjuration instead of becoming a
separate fiction project.

## Open Questions

- Which genre sourcebooks are most useful for this mode: fantasy, sci-fi,
  urban mystery, horror, cyberpunk, or weird travelogue?
- Should Conjuration maintain a campaign notebook, a sketchbook, or both?
- How much randomness is useful before the exercise becomes bookkeeping?
- What is the smallest session that produces both written texture and a
  drawable subject?
