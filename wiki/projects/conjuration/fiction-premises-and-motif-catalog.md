# Fiction Premises And Motif Catalog

This page holds raw story seeds after they become reusable premise patterns.
It is not a plot archive. The goal is to preserve engines that can generate
characters, scenes, motifs, world rules, and visual practice.

The useful unit is a premise card:

- core impossibility or pressure;
- social or institutional setting;
- character lens;
- visual anchor;
- escalation path;
- ethical boundary;
- one scene that proves the premise has energy.

## Premise Engines

Several seeds share a pattern: change one social rule, then watch ordinary life
become strange.

Examples:

- physical anonymity in the workplace;
- a society organized around bed-like living modules;
- a school club that turns play, mystery, and social reconnaissance into an
  institution;
- a local dictionary of obscure sorrows, jokes, or emotional states;
- everyday communication artifacts, such as flashcards, becoming a measure of
  whether a life is repeating or expanding.

These premises are useful because they have both a surface image and a social
question. The image makes them drawable. The question makes them writable.

## Speculative Rules

Other seeds start from a speculative rule.

- A magician world can be built by asking what familiar military, industrial,
  or communication functions become under magic.
- A perception-reality story can ask what happens when atypical perception
  changes the world rather than merely interpreting it.
- A "unique skill" frame can translate inner capacities into RPG-like powers,
  classes, incantations, or constraints.

For Conjuration, the useful move is to make the rule concrete enough to test:
what object, costume, room, school, street, or ritual would reveal the rule in
one scene?

## Mythic Inversion

The Prometheus-like seed is a good example of mythic inversion: a heroic gift
also becomes a burden. The premise should not stop at "savior suffers." It
needs pressures around institutions, public image, dependency, obligation, and
the people close enough to see the cost.

This kind of premise can be tested by asking:

- What did the protagonist give the world?
- What punishment or cost keeps compounding?
- Who benefits from keeping the hero visible?
- Who sees the person rather than the symbol?
- What small act of rebellion restores agency?

## Social Proof Thrillers

Some seeds imagine a brilliant or unsettling protagonist staging social
experiments that expose hypocrisy, incentives, or hidden institutional logic.

Handle this carefully. The durable writing value is not procedural escalation.
It is the structure:

- a visible contradiction in society;
- a protagonist who wants to make the contradiction undeniable;
- a foil who understands perception, legitimacy, and social cost;
- public reaction that becomes part of the plot;
- consequences that force the protagonist to confront the difference between
  proof, performance, and harm.

Future drafts should focus on character pressure, moral ambiguity, and
institutional response. Avoid turning premise notes into instructions for real
coercion, deception, or harm.

## Local Texture

The Singaporean-sorrows idea points to a local concept catalog: invented words,
short definitions, jokes, emotional states, and tiny social observations that
could feed essays, stories, comics, or worldbuilding.

Useful pattern:

1. Coin or collect a local term.
2. Define the emotional state.
3. Attach one scene.
4. Add one sensory or linguistic marker.
5. Decide whether it belongs in Chronicle voice practice or Conjuration
   fiction.

This is a bridge to [Writing Practice And Voice](../chronicle/writing-practice-and-voice.md):
some entries are literary miniatures, while others become story seeds.

## Scene Tests

Before a premise becomes a project, test it with a small scene.

- Who is in the room?
- What does each person want before the premise intrudes?
- What object or visual detail makes the premise legible?
- What line of dialogue could only happen in this world?
- What pressure escalates by the end of the scene?

The scene test prevents premise hoarding. If the idea cannot generate one
concrete moment, it probably belongs in the archive rather than the active
Conjuration queue.
