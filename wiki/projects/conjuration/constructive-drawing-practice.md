# Constructive Drawing Practice

Constructive drawing is the practical side of Conjuration: build subjects out
of simple forms, understand them in perspective, previsualize marks before
committing, and use feedback to improve the next attempt.

The project should prefer exercises that strengthen imagination and structure,
not only copying finish-level details.

## Practice Principles

- Construct before rendering.
- Previsualize and ghost before committing to a line.
- Use faint marks, dots, axes, center lines, and bounding boxes to hold the
  intended structure.
- Treat gesture as compression: a small number of marks should preserve the
  important degrees of freedom of a pose or object.
- Check earlier and more deliberately, especially for proportion and
  perspective mistakes.
- Prefer subjects that expose the current weakness rather than subjects that
  produce appealing drawings.

This turns drawing into an explicit feedback loop rather than an open-ended
session of making marks.

## Session Shape

A useful weeknight session can be short but structured:

1. Choose one target: line quality, form intersection, animal simplification,
   portrait block-in, perspective scene, or invented creature construction.
2. Write the constraint and the intended check before drawing.
3. Warm up with simple forms or lines.
4. Do a focused study from observation, reference, or a 3D model.
5. Do an imagination application using the same structure.
6. Check the result against reference, model, overlay, or proportional marks.
7. Record what failed, what improved, and what the next session should isolate.

The key move is separating study from application. The study supplies structure;
the imagination pass tests whether the structure is usable without direct
copying.

## Subject Tracks

Current source notes suggest several candidate tracks.

Pokemon-like or monster construction is a good constrained track because it
combines simple primitives, creature appeal, perspective, and invented design.
It can scale from toy-like forms to more complex animals.

Portrait construction exposes block-in order, measurement, cranium and cheek
structure, and the gap between visual copying and constructive understanding.

Figure and gesture drawing can be treated as compression: capture the pose,
perspective hints, and form relationships with minimal marks, then expand only
where structure supports it.

Scene-embedded construction asks for imaginary objects, characters, or
creatures to be drawn into an existing scene, grid, room, or camera view. This
uses real perspective as scaffolding.

## Feedback Sources

Conjuration needs ground truth because visualization practice alone is easy to
fake.

- Observation: draw a real object after predicting its unseen angle.
- Reference: hide the reference, draw from memory, then reveal and correct.
- Overlay: draw over a hidden copy of a reference to isolate where errors
  entered.
- 3D tools: use Blender, Sketchfab models, or simple camera setups to create
  checkable views.
- Physical models: use toys, hands, simple objects, or clay as imagination
  triggers.
- Digital sketch tools: use drawing layers, perspective grids, or 3D sketching
  apps when they make the feedback loop tighter.

The tool is secondary. The important thing is that the exercise has a planned
comparison.

## Museum And Reference Prompts

Museum notes can become drawing prompts when they are treated as structured
observation rather than vague inspiration.

Useful prompts:

- study how an object becomes memorable through silhouette, material, use, and
  named detail;
- imagine a future museum exhibit and draw the artifact, label, and display
  context;
- compare how different paintings or sculptures stylize the same human form;
- redraw a familiar room as if it were a curated exhibit;
- ask how a graphic-design piece moves the viewer's gaze;
- extract one motif from an artwork and recombine it with an invented subject.

This keeps museum visits connected to constructive practice: notice the form,
compress the observation, then turn it into a checkable sketch or design
constraint.

## Open Questions

- Which subject track should become the first serious curriculum?
- What is the right balance between casual volume and measured correction?
- Are 3D tools helping internal visualization, or outsourcing it?
- Which errors appear first: proportion, perspective, construction, line
  confidence, or memory loss?
